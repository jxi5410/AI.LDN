Fifteen Papers That Built Modern AI

The Most Influential Papers of the Deep Learning Era

The entire AI revolution traces back to a surprisingly small group of people.

They keep showing up in each other’s papers, co-founding each other’s companies, and competing against their former collaborators. Sutskever co-authored AlexNet, co-founded OpenAI, then started a $32B company. Kaplan wrote the Scaling Laws paper, then co-founded Anthropic (~$380B). The same names keep appearing — on different sides of the table each time.

I set out to research the most influential papers behind modern AI. As a non-technical researcher, I wasn’t after the maths — I wanted the human stories. What I found was far more fascinating than I expected: origin myths stranger than fiction, eerie coincidences, and a surprisingly tangled web connecting all of them. Here are the fifteen paradigm-defining papers.

They span computer vision, language, reinforcement learning, generative models, multimodal learning, and the science of scaling itself — and together, they tell the story of how we got here.

A note: Ilya Sutskever, co-founder of OpenAI, once gave John Carmack (the legendary creator of Doom) a list of 27 papers to read to “very quickly become super smart on AI.” Several of the papers below appear on that list. This is our version — told through the people, not the proofs.

1. LeNet — The grandfather of it all

“Gradient-Based Learning Applied to Document Recognition” Yann LeCun, Léon Bottou, Yoshua Bengio, Patrick Haffner · AT&T Bell Labs · 1998

The idea in plain English: Before LeNet, computers “read” handwritten text using hand-crafted rules — essentially a massive list of “if this, then that” conditions. LeCun’s insight was to let a neural network learn to read by itself. LeNet-5 was the first practical convolutional neural network: it looked at a handwritten digit through a series of learned filters — first detecting edges, then curves, then whole shapes — progressively building up understanding like a child learning to recognise letters. The key innovations — convolutional layers, pooling layers, hierarchical feature extraction — remain the foundation of all modern computer vision.

Why it changed everything: By 1998, LeNet processed more than 10% of all checks deposited in the United States. Some ATMs still run code that LeCun and Bottou wrote in the 1990s. The paper also introduced MNIST — the “Hello World” dataset that virtually every machine learning practitioner has used. With ~58,000 citations and still accumulating 5,000+ per year nearly three decades later, it’s one of the most enduring papers in computer science. But the deeper significance is that it proved neural networks could work on real-world problems at industrial scale — a fact the field then promptly forgot during the AI winter of the 2000s, when support vector machines became fashionable and CNNs were considered expensive and unnecessary.

What it built: The entire CNN paradigm. Every image recognition system, every autonomous vehicle perception stack, every face recognition model traces its conceptual lineage to LeNet. The paper’s architectural principles — local connectivity, weight sharing, spatial hierarchies — remain essentially unchanged in modern networks.

Where the authors went: Yann LeCun joined NYU in 2003, was hired personally by Mark Zuckerberg in 2013 to lead Facebook AI Research, won the 2018 Turing Award alongside Bengio and Hinton (the “three godfathers of deep learning”), and in November 2025 left Meta to found Advanced Machine Intelligence Labs (AMI), raising $1.03 billion at a $3.5B valuation. Yoshua Bengio won the same Turing Award, became the most-cited living scientist across all fields (1M+ Google Scholar citations), and now leads global AI safety efforts as co-president of LawZero.

The stories behind the paper: LeCun, French-born, became interested in AI from reading about the Piaget vs. Chomsky debate on whether intelligence is learned or innate. He independently developed backpropagation in 1985 — the same technique that would power the entire deep learning revolution — then did a postdoc with Geoffrey Hinton, forging one of AI’s most important collaborations. At AT&T Bell Labs (the legendary institution that invented the transistor), he built LeNet during an era of bold, curiosity-driven research. Then came the AI winter: CNNs worked but were computationally expensive, and the field moved on. LeCun’s work was largely ignored for over a decade. The vindication arc is one of the great stories in science — from obscurity to the Turing Award to founding a billion-dollar company. He’s also famous for his colourful personality: jazz saxophonist, drone builder, outspoken social media presence, and prolific poster of hot takes on X.

2. ImageNet — The dataset that ignited everything

“ImageNet: A Large Scale Hierarchical Image Database” Jia Deng, Wei Dong, Richard Socher, Li-Jia Li, Kai Li, Fei-Fei Li · Stanford / Princeton · 2009

The idea in plain English: While everyone else in AI was obsessing over better algorithms, a Stanford professor named Fei-Fei Li had a contrarian insight: the bottleneck wasn’t algorithms — it was data. ImageNet is a database of over 14 million hand-annotated images across 22,000+ categories, organised using WordNet’s semantic hierarchy (a linguistic database that maps how words relate to each other). The categories range from “dalmatian” to “mushroom” to “suspension bridge.” Each image was labeled by human workers on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The accompanying annual competition — the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC, 2010–2017) — became the proving ground where deep learning demonstrated its superiority over hand-crafted approaches.

Why it changed everything: The ILSVRC competition directly produced AlexNet’s 2012 breakthrough (a 10.8% margin over the runner-up), followed by VGGNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet. By 2015, machines surpassed estimated human performance on the challenge (3.57% vs ~5.1% error). Without ImageNet, there is no AlexNet. Without AlexNet, there is no deep learning revolution. The dataset paper has ~69,700 citations — one of the most cited in all of computer science.

What it built: The ILSVRC competition was the crucible for nearly every major computer vision architecture of the 2010s. ImageNet pre-trained models became the default starting point for transfer learning across medical imaging, autonomous driving, satellite analysis, agriculture, and industrial inspection. The “ImageNet moment” — when a field gets its own large-scale benchmark dataset that unlocks rapid progress — became a template that NLP (with GLUE/SuperGLUE), speech (LibriSpeech), and other fields explicitly tried to replicate.

Where the authors went: Fei-Fei Li co-founded Stanford HAI (Human-Centered AI Institute), co-founded AI4ALL (promoting diversity in AI), and founded World Labs (spatial intelligence, raised $230M). She was named TIME’s 2025 Person of the Year. Richard Socher went on to co-found MetaMind (acquired by Salesforce for ~$32.8M), served as Salesforce’s Chief Scientist, then founded You.com (AI search). Jia Deng became a professor at Princeton and later the University of Michigan.

The stories behind the paper: Fei-Fei Li’s personal story is one of the most remarkable in AI. She immigrated from China to New Jersey as a teenager, her family arriving with less than $20. Her parents ran a dry-cleaning business; she worked in Chinese restaurants to help the family survive. She won a full scholarship to Princeton — so unexpected that she had two advisors verify the acceptance letter. Her high school math teacher Bob Sabella and his wife lent her parents money for the business. From that starting point, she built the dataset that ignited the entire modern AI era. When she first proposed ImageNet, the research community was deeply skeptical — the project seemed absurdly ambitious and the focus on data rather than algorithms went against the grain. She made the entire dataset freely available, a decision that altered the trajectory of AI research. Her 2023 memoir, The Worlds I See, became a bestseller.

A visualisation of ImageNet — Andrej Karpathy’s embedding of ImageNet’s 1,000 categories, showing how the dataset maps the visual world into a structured hierarchy.

3. AlexNet — The shot heard round the world

“ImageNet Classification with Deep Convolutional Neural Networks” Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, Geoffrey Hinton · University of Toronto · 2012

The idea in plain English: Before 2012, teaching a computer to recognise objects in photos was painstaking, manual work. Researchers hand-designed mathematical features — edge detectors, gradient histograms, texture descriptors — and fed them into shallow classifiers. It was like trying to teach someone to recognise a cat by giving them a checklist of fur patterns. AlexNet flipped this on its head. Instead of telling the machine what to look for, Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton built a deep neural network with eight learned layers and 60 million parameters, pointed it at 1.2 million labeled images, and let it figure out the features itself. The network learned to see — first edges, then textures, then object parts, then whole objects — all without being told what any of them were. The trick that made it work: training on two consumer NVIDIA GTX 580 graphics cards, each with just 3 GB of memory, using a then-novel activation function called ReLU that trained several times faster than the alternatives.

Why it changed everything: AlexNet won the 2012 ImageNet competition with a top-5 error rate of 15.3% — crushing the runner-up by over ten percentage points. That margin was unheard of. Overnight, the entire computer vision community pivoted from hand-crafted features to neural networks. The paper has been cited over 170,000 times. But the deeper impact was proving the GPU-neural network combination. Every AI chip, every data centre rack of H100s, every trillion-dollar valuation of NVIDIA traces a direct line back to two graphics cards in Alex Krizhevsky’s bedroom.

What it built: Google Photos, autonomous vehicle perception systems, medical imaging diagnostics, and every convolutional neural network that followed — VGGNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet. It also catalysed NVIDIA’s pivot from a gaming company to the engine of the AI economy.

Where the authors went: Geoffrey Hinton shared the 2018 Turing Award and won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics — then resigned from Google to warn the world about AI risks. Ilya Sutskever co-founded OpenAI, served as its Chief Scientist through the GPT era and the dramatic November 2023 board crisis, then left to found Safe Superintelligence Inc. (SSI), now valued at $32 billion. Alex Krizhevsky, the quietest of the three, joined Google, later said he “lost interest,” and is now a venture partner at Two Bear Capital.

The stories behind the paper: AlexNet was literally trained in Krizhevsky’s childhood bedroom at his parents’ house. After it won ImageNet, Hinton formed a shell company called DNNresearch — three employees, no product, no revenue — and auctioned it off in a Lake Tahoe hotel room. Google, Microsoft, Baidu, and DeepMind bid against each other. Near midnight, with the price at $44 million, Hinton paused the bidding and went to sleep. Google won. When splitting the proceeds, Sutskever and Krizhevsky insisted Hinton take the largest share. “It tells you what kind of people they are,” Hinton later said, “not what kind of person I am.”

4. GANs — Two networks walk into a bar

“Generative Adversarial Nets” Ian Goodfellow, Jean Pouget-Abadie, Mehdi Mirza, Bing Xu, David Warde-Farley, Sherjil Ozair, Aaron Courville, Yoshua Bengio · Université de Montréal · 2014

The idea in plain English: Imagine a counterfeiter and a detective locked in an escalating arms race. The counterfeiter produces fake currency; the detective tries to spot it. Every time the detective catches a flaw, the counterfeiter fixes it. Every time the counterfeiter gets better, the detective sharpens their eye. Eventually, the counterfeiter’s work becomes indistinguishable from the real thing. That’s a GAN. One neural network (the Generator) creates synthetic data — images, audio, text — from random noise. A second network (the Discriminator) tries to tell real data from fakes. They train simultaneously in a minimax game, each pushing the other to improve. At equilibrium, the generator produces output so realistic that the discriminator is reduced to coin-flipping.

Why it changed everything: Before GANs, generative models produced blurry, unconvincing outputs. GANs shattered that ceiling. By 2018, NVIDIA’s StyleGAN was generating photorealistic human faces that fooled essentially everyone — spawning the viral website “This Person Does Not Exist.” Yann LeCun called adversarial training “the most interesting idea in the last 10 years in ML.” The paper has been cited roughly 75,000–100,000 times. GANs also ignited difficult conversations. Deepfakes — GAN-generated face swaps — became a major security and misinformation concern.

What it built: StyleGAN (photorealistic faces), CycleGAN (style transfer between image domains), Pix2Pix (image-to-image translation), SRGAN (super-resolution), deepfake technology, and a vast ecosystem of creative tools. Though diffusion models eventually overtook GANs for image generation, the adversarial training principle remains influential.

Where the authors went: Ian Goodfellow — known in the field as “The GANfather” — bounced between Google Brain, OpenAI, Google again, then Apple, where he famously quit over the return-to-office policy in 2022. He’s now a principal scientist at Google DeepMind and co-authored the definitive Deep Learning textbook with Bengio and Courville. Yoshua Bengio won the 2018 Turing Award, became the most-cited living scientist across all fields, and now leads global efforts on AI safety.

The stories behind the paper: The entire concept was born in a single evening. In late May 2014, Goodfellow was drinking at Les 3 Brasseurs in Montreal, celebrating a friend’s graduation. Fellow PhD students were struggling with a generative modelling problem. Goodfellow suggested using a discriminator network to guide a generator — then went home and coded the first GAN that same night in about an hour while his girlfriend slept. It produced recognisable handwritten digits within minutes. The next morning, he emailed the lab: “Who wants to write a NIPS paper with me?” Lab members responded like the fellowship from Lord of the Rings: “You have my sword.” “You have my bow.” They wrote the entire paper in two weeks. When colleague Bing Xu generated the first face images, Goodfellow recalled: “The results looked like crap. But we had been looking at crap from generative models for years, and we could tell that this was an exciting, new kind of crap.”

5. ResNet — Going a thousand layers deep without getting lost

“Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition” Kaiming He, Xiangyu Zhang, Shaoqing Ren, Jian Sun · Microsoft Research Asia · 2015

The idea in plain English: By 2015, everyone knew deeper neural networks were better — in theory. In practice, stacking more than about 20 layers caused networks to degrade. Not from overfitting, but from a fundamental optimisation problem: gradients (the signals that teach the network) faded to nothing as they travelled backward through dozens of layers. It was as if you were trying to whisper a message through a chain of a hundred people, and by the middle, no one could hear anything. Kaiming He’s insight was elegant and almost embarrassingly simple: skip connections. Instead of asking each block of layers to learn the full transformation, let it learn only the difference — the residual — from what came in. A shortcut wire carries the original signal straight through, and the layers just learn what to add or subtract. It’s the neural network equivalent of “start with what you’ve got and make small adjustments.”

Why it changed everything: ResNet swept the 2015 ImageNet competition, winning first place in all five main tracks with a 152-layer network that achieved a 3.57% error rate — surpassing estimated human performance of 5.1%. Nature ranked it the most cited paper of the 21st century across all scientific fields — not just computer science, but all of science — with over 250,000 citations. More fundamentally, skip connections became a universal design principle. Every Transformer model — GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA — uses residual connections. AlphaFold uses them. Diffusion models use them. The idea is now so pervasive it’s like oxygen: invisible but essential.

What it built: ResNet-50 and its variants remain production workhorses for image classification, object detection, and medical imaging worldwide. The residual connection principle underpins essentially every modern deep architecture.

Where the authors went: Kaiming He moved to Facebook AI Research, created Mask R-CNN and MAE, then joined MIT as a tenured professor in 2025 while also serving as a Distinguished Scientist at Google DeepMind. He holds over 803,000 citations — fifth globally across all fields. His co-author Jian Sun co-founded Megvii (Face++), one of China’s largest AI companies, but tragically passed away in June 2022 at age 45.

The stories behind the paper: He scored first in all of Guangdong Province on China’s college entrance exam in 2003. Twelve years later, his network scored first in the world’s biggest computer vision competition. Even the authors were stunned — co-author Jian Sun told Microsoft: “We even didn’t believe this single idea could be so significant.” A 2023 study of the fruit fly larva brain published in Science later discovered that biological neural circuits contain “multilayer shortcuts” eerily similar to residual connections — nature arrived at the same solution independently.

The residual block — input x bypasses two weight layers via a shortcut connection: y = F(x) + x. The most reproduced diagram in all of deep learning.

6. Attention Is All You Need — The architecture behind everything

“Attention Is All You Need” Ashish Vaswani, Noam Shazeer, Niki Parmar, Jakob Uszkoreit, Llion Jones, Aidan N. Gomez, Łukasz Kaiser, Illia Polosukhin · Google Brain / Google Research · 2017

The idea in plain English: Before 2017, language-processing AI worked like reading a book one word at a time, left to right, trying to remember what came before. These recurrent neural networks were sequential and slow — each word had to wait for the previous word to be processed, and long-range connections faded like a game of telephone. The Transformer eliminated all of this. Instead of reading sequentially, it processes every word in a sentence simultaneously, computing a map of how much every word relates to every other word — a mechanism called self-attention. It’s the difference between reading a book word by word and seeing the entire page at once, with glowing threads connecting every word to the words that matter most to its meaning. This allowed massive parallelisation on GPUs, making training orders of magnitude faster.

Why it changed everything: The Transformer didn’t just improve on the status quo. It became the status quo. Every major AI system in the world today — GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, BERT, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, AlphaFold, Whisper, Sora — is built on the Transformer architecture. Cited over 173,000 times, the paper is the architectural blueprint of the entire generative AI era.

What it built: ChatGPT. Google Search (via BERT). GitHub Copilot. Google Translate. Claude. Gemini. Midjourney. Stable Diffusion. Sora. Whisper. AlphaFold. Vision Transformers. Virtually every frontier AI product traces its lineage here.

Where the authors went: All eight authors were at Google; seven of the eight eventually left to start companies. Noam Shazeer co-founded Character.AI, then returned to Google to co-lead Gemini in a deal worth $2.7 billion. Aidan Gomez, who was an undergraduate intern at the time, co-founded Cohere, now valued at $6.8 billion. Ashish Vaswani and Niki Parmar co-founded Essential AI. Jakob Uszkoreit founded Inceptive (RNA therapeutics). Llion Jones co-founded Sakana AI in Tokyo. Illia Polosukhin co-founded NEAR Protocol. Only Łukasz Kaiser joined OpenAI instead of starting a company. The combined value of the authors’ ventures exceeds $10 billion.

The stories behind the paper: The title is a Beatles reference — “All You Need Is Love.” The name “Transformer” was chosen by Jakob Uszkoreit because he simply liked how it sounded; an early internal document included illustrations of Hasbro Transformers robots. The idea started as a hallway conversation at Google’s Mountain View campus. Uszkoreit’s father, the eminent computational linguist Hans Uszkoreit, was openly sceptical that attention without recurrence could work. The original base model trained in just 12 hours on 8 NVIDIA P100 GPUs — a setup that would be quaint by today’s standards yet produced the architecture that powers trillion-dollar industries.

7. BERT — Reading in both directions at once

“BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding” Jacob Devlin, Ming-Wei Chang, Kenton Lee, Kristina Toutanova · Google AI Language · 2018

The idea in plain English: GPT (which preceded BERT by a few months) read text left to right — predicting the next word based only on what came before. BERT asked: what if the model could see words on both sides simultaneously? Imagine filling in a blank in the sentence “The ___ sat on the mat.” A left-to-right model can only use “The.” BERT uses both “The” and “sat on the mat” — the full context. To achieve this, BERT introduced Masked Language Modelling: randomly hiding 15% of the words in a passage and training the model to guess them from surrounding context — essentially a massive, automated game of fill-in-the-blank played over billions of sentences.

Why it changed everything: BERT set new records on 11 NLP benchmarks simultaneously, surpassing human performance on the Stanford Question Answering Dataset. Google called it “the biggest leap forward in the past five years, and one of the biggest leaps forward in the history of Search.” By 2020, virtually every English-language Google search was processed by a BERT model. It established the “pre-train then fine-tune” paradigm that dominated NLP for years.

What it built: Google Search (billions of queries daily), plus an enormous family of successors: RoBERTa, DistilBERT, ALBERT, XLNet, ERNIE, BioBERT, and many more.

Where the authors went: Jacob Devlin continued at Google, briefly left for OpenAI in 2023, then returned. Kristina Toutanova joined Google DeepMind and holds over 191,000 citations.

The stories behind the paper: BERT is named after the Sesame Street character, a playful nod to ELMo (Embeddings from Language Models), an earlier model named after Elmo. This launched a delightful tradition of “Muppetware” in NLP: ERNIE, Big Bird, Grover, Kermit, RoBERTa. Google open-sourced BERT’s code and pre-trained weights within weeks — an unusually fast release that turbocharged worldwide adoption.

BERT’s pre-train then fine-tune paradigm — pre-train on massive text with masked language modelling, then fine-tune on specific tasks with minimal additional data.

8. Scaling Laws — The power laws that launched an arms race

“Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models” Jared Kaplan, Sam McCandlish, Tom Henighan, Tom B. Brown, Benjamin Chess, Rewon Child, Scott Gray, Alec Radford, Jeffrey Wu, Dario Amodei · OpenAI / Johns Hopkins · 2020

The idea in plain English: What if you could predict exactly how smart your AI would be before you trained it — just by knowing how big you planned to make it? That’s what this paper discovered. By systematically training language models across seven orders of magnitude in size, data, and compute, Kaplan and colleagues found that performance follows clean, predictable power-law relationships. On a log-log graph, the curves are straight lines. Double the parameters, and loss drops by a predictable amount. Architecture details — width, depth, number of attention heads — barely matter once you hold total parameter count fixed. The single most effective thing you can do: make the model bigger.

Why it changed everything: This paper transformed LLM development from alchemy into engineering. Before it, scaling decisions were guesswork. After it, labs could mathematically forecast the return on a billion dollars of compute. It directly justified the massive bet on GPT-3 and gave strategic confidence to every subsequent frontier model. The paper was later partially corrected by DeepMind’s Chinchilla (2022), which showed data should scale equally with parameters — meaning GPT-3 was dramatically undertrained. Never formally peer-reviewed, yet one of the decade’s most consequential papers.

What it built: GPT-3, GPT-4, Claude, Chinchilla, LLaMA, and the planning methodology behind every frontier model.

Where the authors went: Of the ten authors, at least five co-founded Anthropic in 2021 — making this paper the incubator for a company now valued at roughly $380 billion. Jared Kaplan, a theoretical physicist who spent 15 years studying quantum gravity before pivoting to AI, became Anthropic’s Chief Science Officer (net worth estimated at $3.7 billion by Forbes). Dario Amodei became CEO. Sam McCandlish became CTO.

The stories behind the paper: Kaplan’s doctoral thesis was titled “Aspects of Holography,” advised by the legendary physicist Nima Arkani-Hamed at Harvard. His training in finding mathematical regularities in the physical universe directly informed his approach to finding power laws in neural networks.

The scaling laws triptych — test loss vs. compute, dataset size, and parameters on log-log plots. Remarkably clean straight lines spanning seven orders of magnitude.

9. GPT-3 — The 175-billion-parameter leap of faith

“Language Models are Few-Shot Learners” Tom B. Brown, Benjamin Mann, Nick Ryder, and 28 co-authors · OpenAI · 2020

The idea in plain English: Before GPT-3, using a language model for a specific task meant fine-tuning it — training it further on task-specific data. GPT-3 showed this was unnecessary. At 175 billion parameters — ten times larger than any previous dense model — it could perform tasks it had never been explicitly trained for, simply by reading a few examples in its input prompt. Show it three examples of English-to-French translation, then give it an English sentence, and it translates. This “in-context learning” meant the model was learning at inference time, picking up patterns from a handful of examples and generalising on the spot.

Why it changed everything: GPT-3 validated the scaling hypothesis with dramatic force. It launched the API-as-a-product model for AI, spawned thousands of startups, and directly led to ChatGPT — which reached 100 million users in two months, the fastest-growing consumer application in history. The 75-page paper also birthed the entire discipline of prompt engineering.

What it built: ChatGPT, GPT-4, the OpenAI API, Codex, GitHub Copilot, Microsoft Copilot, DALL-E, Jasper AI, and the entire generative AI startup ecosystem. Every major competitor — Google’s PaLM/Gemini, Meta’s LLaMA, Anthropic’s Claude — was built in direct response.

Where the authors went: The paper’s author list reads like a roster of AI industry leadership. Dario Amodei (VP of Research) co-founded Anthropic as CEO. Tom Brown (lead author), Jared Kaplan, Sam McCandlish, and Jack Clark all co-founded Anthropic. Ilya Sutskever later founded SSI. Alec Radford — architect of the entire GPT series — stayed at OpenAI. At least seven of Anthropic’s founding team were GPT-3 co-authors.

The training data bug: a $4.6 million mistake they couldn’t undo

The GPT-3 paper contains one of the most candid admissions in AI history. OpenAI’s decontamination method used a 13-gram overlap approach: they searched for 13-gram overlaps between all benchmark test sets and the training data, then removed colliding sequences plus a 200-character window around them. But there was a bug. The authors wrote:

“Unfortunately, a bug in the filtering caused us to ignore some overlaps, and due to the cost of training it was not feasible to retrain the model.”

The filtering failed specifically on long documents such as books. The contamination was strikingly widespread:

  • Excluded entirely from the paper (too contaminated): Four Wikipedia language modelling benchmarks and the Children’s Book Test were “almost entirely contained” in training data. These results simply don’t appear.
  • Heavily contaminated but judged tolerable: QuAC had only 1% clean examples. SQuAD v2 was only 6% clean. DROP was only 7% clean. Manual inspection showed that source passages appeared in training, but actual question-answer pairs did not.
  • Meaningfully contaminated: PIQA showed 29% contamination and a 3-point performance drop on clean data. Winograd had 45% flagged examples with a 2.6% decrease on clean data. Both were marked with asterisks in the results.

Why couldn’t they just retrain? GPT-3 175B required approximately 355 GPU-years of V100 compute. A single training run cost an estimated $4.6 million. The team had trained eight different model sizes. Retraining was simply economically impossible.

Despite the alarming contamination percentages, the authors concluded that contamination had surprisingly little effect on most results — there was no correlation between contamination level and performance impact. They speculated that the training corpus was so massive that even 175 billion parameters didn’t meaningfully overfit.

The lasting legacy was methodological. The 13-gram overlap detection became the standard decontamination approach adopted by PaLM, LLaMA, and LLaMA 2. The GPT-4 technical report later made a remarkably similar admission: “portions of BIG-bench were inadvertently mixed into the training set.” The bug effectively founded the entire subfield of data contamination research.

GPT-3 scaling curves — task performance smoothly improving across model sizes from 125M to 175B parameters. Few-shot curves climb most steeply, proving bigger was qualitatively different.

10. DQN — Teaching a computer to play videogames (and accidentally selling a company)

“Playing Atari with Deep Reinforcement Learning” / “Human-level control through deep reinforcement learning” Volodymyr Mnih, Koray Kavukcuoglu, David Silver, and co-authors · DeepMind · 2013/2015

The idea in plain English: Imagine sitting a toddler in front of a 1970s Atari console — no instruction manual, no explanation of the rules — and watching them figure out how to play dozens of different games just by watching the screen and keeping score. That’s essentially what the Deep Q-Network did. It received only raw pixels (84×84 images, four frames at a time) and a score, and learned to play 49 different Atari games using the exact same algorithm for every game. No game-specific knowledge. No human guidance beyond the score signal.

Why it changed everything: DQN founded the field of deep reinforcement learning. Published in Nature in 2015, it gave deep learning unprecedented mainstream scientific credibility. The original Atari results were published in December 2013, and one month later, Google acquired DeepMind for reportedly $400–650 million. The DQN demo was explicitly the key demonstration that attracted Google’s interest.

What it built: AlphaGo, AlphaZero, AlphaStar, MuZero, Agent57. Google used DeepMind’s RL techniques to reduce data centre cooling energy by ~40%. The approach influenced autonomous driving, robotics, chip design, and drug discovery.

Where the authors went: David Silver led AlphaGo and won the 2019 ACM Prize in Computing, then in January 2026 left DeepMind to found Ineffable Intelligence. Demis Hassabis steered DeepMind from Atari to AlphaFold, won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, and was knighted. Koray Kavukcuoglu rose to VP of Research at Google DeepMind.

The stories behind the paper: The legendary moment: in the game Breakout, the DQN agent independently discovered the optimal strategy of tunnelling around the side of the brick wall and sending the ball behind to clear blocks from above — a tactic the researchers hadn’t anticipated. This became the most famous demo in deep RL history. Elon Musk was an early DeepMind investor; his frustration over Google acquiring the company reportedly helped motivate him to co-found OpenAI in 2015.

Atari Breakout — the game where DQN independently discovered the tunnel strategy, carving a channel through the brick wall to send the ball behind for rapid clearing.

11. AlphaGo — The game where a machine made a move no human had ever conceived

“Mastering the Game of Go with Deep Neural Networks and Tree Search” David Silver, Aja Huang, and 18 co-authors · Google DeepMind · 2016

The idea in plain English: Go has roughly 10^170 possible board configurations — more than the number of atoms in the observable universe. Brute-force search, which had conquered chess, was hopelessly inadequate. Experts predicted it would take at least another decade for AI to beat a professional player. AlphaGo combined three ideas: a policy network trained on 30 million positions from expert games to predict likely next moves, a value network trained to evaluate who’s winning from any position, and Monte Carlo tree search that used both networks to explore the game tree intelligently.

Why it changed everything: AlphaGo defeated Lee Sedol 4–1 in March 2016 in Seoul. Over 200 million people watched. Professional players began studying AI-suggested moves as standard practice. Lee Sedol retired from professional Go in 2019, partly citing the impossibility of competing with AI. The technical legacy is even larger: AlphaGo’s deep RL + search paradigm directly led to AlphaFold, which solved protein structure prediction and won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

What it built: AlphaGo Zero (beat the original 100–0 using zero human data), AlphaZero (mastered chess in 4 hours of self-play), AlphaStar, MuZero, AlphaFold (200 million predicted protein structures, used by 3 million researchers in 190 countries), and AlphaProof (silver medal at the International Mathematical Olympiad, 2024).

Where the authors went: Demis Hassabis won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, was knighted, and founded Isomorphic Labs for AI-driven drug discovery. David Silver won the 2019 ACM Prize and recently left to found his own startup.

The stories behind the paper: Move 37 is the most iconic moment in AI history. During Game 2, AlphaGo played a shoulder hit on the fifth line — a move that violated centuries of Go wisdom. Fan Hui, watching as commentator, exclaimed: “It’s just bad. We don’t know why. It’s bad!” AlphaGo’s own system estimated the move had a 1 in 10,000 chance of being played by a human. Lee Sedol took over 12 minutes to respond. He later said: “I thought AlphaGo was merely a machine. But when I saw this move, I changed my mind. Surely, AlphaGo is creative.” In Game 4, Lee struck back with his own 1-in-10,000 move — Move 78, “God’s Touch” — exploiting a weakness in AlphaGo’s search algorithm. It was the only game a human has ever won against AlphaGo in a formal match.

AlphaGo’s neural network training pipeline — combining policy networks, value networks, and Monte Carlo tree search to master Go.

12. DDPM — Teaching machines to dream images into existence

“Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models” Jonathan Ho, Ajay Jain, Pieter Abbeel · UC Berkeley · 2020

The idea in plain English: Imagine taking a photograph and gradually adding static — like an old television losing signal — until it becomes pure white noise. Now imagine training a neural network to reverse that process: given a noisy image, predict and remove a tiny bit of noise, recovering a slightly cleaner image. Repeat a thousand times, and you go from pure static to a crisp, coherent photograph that never existed before. The training objective is surprisingly simple: just predict the noise that was added.

Why it changed everything: Before DDPM, GANs dominated image generation but suffered from training instability and mode collapse. Diffusion models were stable, produced diverse outputs, and — crucially — were easily steered by conditioning signals like text descriptions. By 2022, diffusion models had completely displaced GANs as the dominant image generation paradigm. A PhD project at UC Berkeley — not a corporate lab — spawned an entire industry.

What it built: DALL-E 2 (OpenAI), Stable Diffusion (whose core model file is literally named ddpm.py), Midjourney, Imagen (Google), Sora (OpenAI’s video generator), DreamFusion (text-to-3D). The entire ecosystem of AI image and video generation.

Where the authors went: Jonathan Ho joined Google, contributed to Imagen, then co-founded Ideogram AI ($96.5M raised). Ajay Jain co-created DreamFusion and co-founded Genmo AI (video generation). Pieter Abbeel — Andrew Ng’s first PhD student — co-founded Covariant (licensed by Amazon in 2024) and was appointed head of Amazon’s LLM efforts in December 2025. Abbeel’s former students have gone on to co-found OpenAI, Perplexity, Physical Intelligence, Ideogram, and Genmo — an extraordinary lineage.

The stories behind the paper: Jonathan Ho has no Wikipedia page despite sparking the technology behind Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 2, and Midjourney. The original GitHub repository still uses TensorFlow 1.15 and Python 3.5 — charmingly dated code that rewired the creative economy.

The diffusion process — an image progressively dissolving into Gaussian noise (forward) and being reconstructed step by step (reverse). The visual shorthand for generative AI.

13. ViT — An image is worth 16×16 words

“An Image is Worth 16×16 Words: Transformers for Image Recognition at Scale” Alexey Dosovitskiy, Lucas Beyer, Alexander Kolesnikov, Dirk Weissenborn, Xiaohua Zhai, Thomas Unterthiner, Mostafa Dehghani, Matthias Minderer, Georg Heigold, Sylvain Gelly, Jakob Uszkoreit, Neil Houlsby · Google Research / Brain · 2020

The idea in plain English: What if, instead of using convolutional neural networks for images (the standard since AlexNet), you just cut an image into a grid of small patches — like tiles on a floor — and fed them into a Transformer, the same architecture powering GPT and BERT? That’s exactly what ViT does. It divides an image into 16×16-pixel patches, flattens each patch into a vector, adds position information, and passes the whole sequence through a standard Transformer encoder. Each patch becomes a “word” in a visual sentence. The model learns to attend to relationships between patches — how the sky relates to the horizon, how a car door relates to a wheel — using the same self-attention mechanism that helps GPT understand language.

Why it changed everything: ViT proved that pure Transformer architectures could match or exceed CNNs at image recognition when trained at sufficient scale. This triggered a paradigm shift: within two years, Transformers had invaded almost every corner of computer vision, displacing the CNN architectures that had dominated since AlexNet. The paper has approximately 45,000–50,000 citations and was the #1 most-cited AI paper of 2020–2021 by some analyses. The title itself — “An Image is Worth 16×16 Words” — became an iconic phrase in the field.

What it built: Swin Transformer, DeiT, BEiT, MAE, and dozens of Transformer-based vision models now dominating medical imaging, weather prediction, autonomous driving, satellite analysis, and multimodal models like CLIP and DALL-E. Pre-trained ViT models are among the most downloaded on Hugging Face.

Where the authors went: Several authors (including Kolesnikov, Zhai, Beyer) continue as senior researchers at Google DeepMind. Jakob Uszkoreit — who also co-authored the original Transformer paper and chose the name “Transformer” — left Google to found Inceptive (RNA therapeutics design). Neil Houlsby moved to DeepMind and works on efficient model architectures.

The stories behind the paper: The paper was initially met with some scepticism because the conclusion seemed to be “Transformers beat CNNs, but only if you train on a huge private Google dataset (JFT-300M).” When fine-tuned on public data alone, early ViTs underperformed. This led to a flurry of follow-up work (DeiT from Facebook AI, which showed Transformers could match CNNs with clever training on ImageNet alone) and a broader debate about whether the advantage was the architecture or the data scale. The answer turned out to be both — and this debate itself accelerated the field.

14. CLIP — The bridge between seeing and reading

“Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision” Alec Radford, Jong Wook Kim, Chris Hallacy, Aditya Ramesh, Gabriel Goh, Sandhini Agarwal, Girish Sastry, Amanda Askell, Pamela Mishkin, Jack Clark, Gretchen Krueger, Ilya Sutskever · OpenAI · 2021

The idea in plain English: Before CLIP, computer vision models were trained on fixed categories — a model trained to recognise 1,000 ImageNet classes couldn’t recognise a “golden retriever wearing a party hat” unless that exact category existed. CLIP broke this limitation by learning to connect images and text descriptions in a shared space. During training, it was shown 400 million image-text pairs scraped from the internet and learned to match each image with its correct caption (and distinguish it from wrong captions). The result: give it any text description, and it can find or classify images — no fine-tuning required. It’s the difference between a dictionary with fixed entries and a mind that understands what words mean.

Why it changed everything: CLIP became the invisible backbone of the entire AI image generation revolution. It powers the text-image alignment in DALL-E 2, Stable Diffusion, and Midjourney — it’s the component that understands what “a cat astronaut riding a horse on Mars” means visually. Its zero-shot classification ability matched ResNet-50 on ImageNet without using any of its 1.28 million labeled training examples, demonstrating that natural language supervision could replace expensive manual labeling. With 30,000+ citations, it fundamentally reshaped how the field thinks about vision-language connections.

What it built: DALL-E 2 (text-image alignment), Stable Diffusion (conditioning), Midjourney, multimedia search engines, content moderation systems, recommendation algorithms, and the entire text-to-image pipeline. The open-source release (OpenCLIP) enabled an explosion of downstream research and products.

Where the authors went: Alec Radford (also the architect of the GPT series) remains at OpenAI as a senior researcher. Aditya Ramesh went on to create DALL-E and DALL-E 2. Ilya Sutskever later co-founded SSI. Amanda Askell moved to Anthropic, where she leads character and personality work on Claude. Jack Clark co-founded Anthropic and now leads its policy work.

The stories behind the paper: CLIP’s training data — 400 million image-text pairs called WIT (WebImageText) — was never publicly released, which became a point of contention. When Stability AI and LAION created their own open dataset (LAION-5B, 5.85 billion pairs) to replicate CLIP’s approach, it triggered a massive debate about data rights, copyright, and the ethics of web scraping that continues to this day. OpenAI’s decision to release the model weights but not the data created an asymmetry: everyone could use CLIP, but only OpenAI (and those with equivalent resources) could train it.

15. Latent Diffusion / Stable Diffusion — Putting AI art on every laptop

“High-Resolution Image Synthesis with Latent Diffusion Models” Robin Rombach, Andreas Blattmann, Dominik Lorenz, Patrick Esser, Björn Ommer · CompVis (LMU Munich) / Runway · 2022

The idea in plain English: DDPM (Paper #12) proved that diffusion models could generate stunning images, but there was a catch: they worked in pixel space, meaning every denoising step processed millions of pixels. This was brutally slow and compute-hungry. Rombach and colleagues had an elegant fix: what if you compressed the image first, did all the denoising in this smaller “latent” space, and then decompressed the result back to full resolution? An encoder squeezes a 512×512 image into a 64×64 latent representation (64× smaller in area), a U-Net performs the diffusion process in this compact space, and a decoder expands the result back to full-resolution pixels. Text conditioning flows in via cross-attention from a CLIP text encoder. The result: comparable quality at roughly 2.7× less compute — enough to run on consumer GPUs with as little as 2.4 GB of VRAM.

Why it changed everything: This paper is the technical foundation of Stable Diffusion, which became the most widely used open-source image generation model in history. During its beta with just 10,000 testers, users generated 1.7 million images per day. Open-sourced in August 2022, it spawned an ecosystem of tools (AUTOMATIC1111, ComfyUI, DreamStudio) that put AI image generation in the hands of millions. It directly triggered the AI art controversy — the Colorado State Fair AI artwork winning first prize, Getty Images lawsuits, and the entire debate about artists’ livelihoods. Outside of ChatGPT, it may be the most culturally visible AI product.

What it built: Stable Diffusion (all versions), Stability AI’s product suite, the entire community of AI art tools, ControlNet (precise conditioning), IP-Adapter, and the open-source generative AI movement. DALL-E 2 and Midjourney used different approaches but validated the same market that Stable Diffusion democratised.

Where the authors went: Robin Rombach and Andreas Blattmann joined Stability AI, where Rombach served as lead researcher. Patrick Esser also joined Stability AI and later became a key contributor to video generation models. Björn Ommer continued as professor at LMU Munich (now at the University of Munich). The business story around Stability AI is dramatic: founder Emad Mostaque raised $101M at a $1B valuation from Coatue and Lightspeed, declaring the goal to “end corporate control over such technologies.” But Mostaque stepped down as CEO in March 2024 amid lawsuits from co-founder Cyrus Hodes, and the company has since struggled with funding challenges.

The stories behind the paper: The paper was built with relatively modest academic resources at LMU Munich’s CompVis group, collaborating with the small startup Runway, while Stability AI provided 4,000 A100 GPUs on AWS for training. The fact that a university research group — not Google, not OpenAI — created the model behind the most widely used AI image generator in history is one of the great David-and-Goliath stories in modern AI. The open-source release was a deliberate ideological choice, standing in direct contrast to OpenAI’s proprietary DALL-E 2, and it fundamentally changed the industry’s relationship with model accessibility.

The latent diffusion pipeline — encoder compresses to latent space, U-Net denoises in compressed space, decoder expands back to pixels. This is why Stable Diffusion runs on consumer GPUs.

What connects these fifteen papers

A few patterns emerge when you step back and look at this collection as a whole.

Simplicity wins. The most enduring ideas are almost embarrassingly simple once stated: skip connections (ResNet), attention without recurrence (Transformers), two networks playing a game (GANs), reverse the noise (diffusion), compress then denoise (latent diffusion), cut the image into patches (ViT). The complexity is in making them work, not in the core concept. Jian Sun’s confession about ResNet — “We didn’t believe this single idea could be so significant” — could apply to nearly every paper on this list.

Data might matter more than algorithms. Fei-Fei Li’s contrarian bet — that the bottleneck was data, not models — turned out to be one of the most prescient insights in the field. ImageNet enabled AlexNet. Internet-scale text enabled GPT-3. 400 million image-text pairs enabled CLIP. LAION-5B enabled Stable Diffusion. The scaling laws paper formalised this: more data → predictably better performance. The researchers who built great datasets were just as important as those who built great architectures.

Papers create companies, companies create industries. The Transformer paper’s eight authors spawned ventures worth over $10 billion. The GPT-3 and Scaling Laws teams seeded Anthropic, now valued at ~$380 billion. The DQN demo sold DeepMind to Google for half a billion dollars. AlexNet’s three authors auctioned themselves off for $44 million. LeNet’s creator raised $1 billion for AMI Labs in 2025. Latent Diffusion’s academic team built the technology behind a company that peaked at a $4 billion valuation.

The talent network is shockingly small. Ilya Sutskever appears on both AlexNet and the GPT papers, and co-authored CLIP. Dario Amodei and Jared Kaplan co-authored both Scaling Laws and GPT-3 before co-founding Anthropic. David Silver and Demis Hassabis co-founded a video game company as students. Kaiming He co-authored both ResNet and Faster R-CNN. Jakob Uszkoreit co-authored both the Transformer and ViT. The entire field pivots on a network of perhaps a few hundred people who keep appearing in each other’s acknowledgments.

The path from paper to product keeps accelerating. LeNet (1998) took over a decade to be vindicated. AlexNet (2012) took years to permeate industry. BERT (2018) reached Google Search in one year. GPT-3 (2020) had an API within months. Latent Diffusion (2022) went from academic paper to Stable Diffusion — used by millions — in months. The gap between “interesting result” and “product used by hundreds of millions” has collapsed, and these fifteen papers are the reason why.

The origin stories are wildly varied. A childhood bedroom (AlexNet). A Montreal bar (GANs). A hallway conversation at Google (Transformers). A dry-cleaning family’s immigrant daughter (ImageNet). A theoretical physicist studying quantum gravity (Scaling Laws). A UC Berkeley PhD project with no Wikipedia page for its lead author (DDPM). A modest Munich university lab outpacing Google and OpenAI (Latent Diffusion). A chess prodigy who designed videogames at 17 (DeepMind/DQN). There is no single path to a paradigm shift — but there is a common thread: every one of these researchers bet on an idea the establishment thought wouldn’t work.

A note on how science actually works

This article is full of origin stories — a bar in Montreal, a bedroom in Toronto, a midnight auction in Lake Tahoe. They’re true, and they’re fun to tell. But they risk creating a misleading impression: that breakthroughs arrive as bolts from the blue, conjured by lone geniuses in moments of inspiration.

The reality is messier, slower, and more collective.

Goodfellow coded the first GAN in an hour — but only because he’d spent years studying generative models under Yoshua Bengio, who’d spent decades developing the mathematical foundations of deep learning. AlexNet was trained in a bedroom — but it ran on ImageNet, which took Fei-Fei Li and her team years to build, drawing on WordNet, which Princeton linguists had been constructing since 1985. The Transformer was born from a hallway conversation — but self-attention had been explored for years before Vaswani and colleagues figured out how to make it the only mechanism needed. The Scaling Laws paper found clean power laws — but only because Kaplan brought 15 years of theoretical physics training to a question no one in ML had thought to ask in that way.

This pattern repeats throughout the history of science. Discoveries are often attributed to those who synthesise and apply — Watson and Crick with DNA, Doudna and Charpentier with CRISPR — rather than those who first identified the underlying phenomena (Friedrich Miescher isolated nucleic acids in 1869; Yoshizumi Ishino observed CRISPR sequences in 1987). Even the vocabulary we use — “bit” (coined by John Tukey, not Claude Shannon), “genetics” (William Bateson, not Gregor Mendel), “survival of the fittest” (Herbert Spencer, not Charles Darwin) — is often contributed by contemporaries and retroactively woven into the narrative.

Thomas Kuhn argued that paradigm shifts don’t emerge from isolated brilliance. They depend on accumulated infrastructure: decades of prior work, competitive exchange between research groups, mathematical tools developed for entirely different purposes, and the slow, unglamorous labour of building datasets, writing proofs, and debugging code at 3am.

None of this diminishes what these fifteen papers achieved. If anything, it makes the achievements more impressive — not less. These researchers didn’t just have a good idea. They recognised which ideas from a vast landscape of prior work could be combined, scaled, and made practical. That act of synthesis, timing, and execution is itself a form of genius.

The stories in this article are entry points, not the full picture. Behind every midnight auction and bar napkin moment, there are years of quiet, incremental work by hundreds of researchers whose names don’t appear in the headlines. They deserve recognition too.

My curiosity for AI grows deeper by the day. Reading through the stories behind these papers — the origin myths, the tangled connections, the sheer improbability of how it all came together — was genuinely one of the most interesting things I’ve done recently.

I hope you find it as fascinating as I did.
